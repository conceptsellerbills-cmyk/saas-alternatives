# Trello vs Notion 2025: Which Tool Fits Your Workflow?

Trello and Notion are both wildly popular tools with millions of users — but they're often compared despite being fundamentally different products. Understanding what each actually does best will save you from the frustration of using the wrong tool for your workflow.

## The Core Difference

**Trello** is a kanban board tool. It's designed to visualize work as cards moving through stages on a board. Simple, visual, immediately intuitive.

**Notion** is a document and database tool. It's designed as an all-in-one workspace where documents, notes, databases, wikis, and task tracking coexist in a flexible hierarchy.

Comparing them directly is like comparing a whiteboard to a notebook — both are useful; they're just for different things.

## Trello: What It Does Well

Trello was built on a single core concept: kanban boards. Lists (columns) represent stages; cards represent work items; dragging cards between columns represents progress. This visual simplicity is Trello's enduring strength.

**Trello excels at:**
- Visualizing workflow stages at a glance
- Simple task tracking for individuals and small teams
- Project status visible to all stakeholders without training
- Agile and kanban workflows
- Automations (Butler) for routine card movements

**Trello's limitations:**
- Limited to board/card structure (everything must fit this model)
- Not designed for long-form documentation
- Searching across multiple boards is awkward
- Database functionality is minimal
- Not suitable for complex project hierarchies

**Best Trello use cases:**
- Software sprint boards
- Content editorial calendars (in progress / in review / published)
- Personal to-do management
- Simple team task tracking
- Visual workflow management

## Notion: What It Does Well

Notion takes a completely different approach: every page is a flexible document that can contain text, embedded databases, kanban boards, tables, galleries, timelines, and more. You can build a Trello-like kanban board in Notion — but also a wiki, a CRM, a content calendar, meeting notes, and a team handbook, all interconnected.

**Notion excels at:**
- Long-form writing and documentation
- Company wikis and knowledge bases
- Combining notes, tasks, databases, and documents
- Linking related information across pages
- SOPs, onboarding docs, and reference materials

**Notion's limitations:**
- Steeper learning curve than Trello
- Can become over-engineered without discipline
- Offline mode is limited
- Performance can lag with very large databases
- Heavier than Trello for simple task tracking

**Best Notion use cases:**
- Company wikis and documentation hubs
- Personal knowledge management systems
- All-in-one workspace combining notes and tasks
- Product specs and project documentation
- Team handbooks and onboarding

## When Trello Wins

**You want immediate visual simplicity.** A new team member can understand a Trello board in 90 seconds. Notion requires exploration.

**Your workflow is primarily kanban.** If you think in stages (To Do / In Progress / Done), Trello's native paradigm is perfect and requires no setup.

**You have non-technical stakeholders.** Clients and external partners can view and update Trello boards with zero learning curve.

**You need a quick, low-commitment project board.** Trello can be operational in 10 minutes.

## When Notion Wins

**You need documentation alongside task management.** Notion holds the brief, the tasks, the meeting notes, and the decision log all in one linked page.

**You're building a team knowledge base.** Notion's wiki functionality is its sweet spot; Trello has no equivalent.

**You have multiple interconnected projects.** Notion's databases can link across projects and provide filtered views; Trello boards are more siloed.

**You want one tool instead of five.** Notion partially replaces Google Docs, Confluence, Trello, and your notes app.

## Pricing Comparison

**Trello:**
- Free: Unlimited cards, 10 boards per workspace, basic Power-Ups
- Standard: $5/user/month — unlimited boards, advanced features
- Premium: $10/user/month — views, unlimited automations, dashboards
- Enterprise: Custom

**Notion:**
- Free: Unlimited pages for personal use; limited team features
- Plus: $10/user/month — unlimited blocks for teams, collaborative workspaces
- Business: $15/user/month — SAML SSO, advanced permissions, analytics
- Enterprise: Custom

At equivalent feature levels, pricing is comparable. Trello's free tier is generous for small teams; Notion's free tier is better for individual users.

## Using Both Together (The Power Combo)

Many teams use both tools for different purposes:

- **Notion** for documentation: meeting notes, project briefs, company wiki, decisions log
- **Trello** for task tracking: the actual status board that shows where each piece of work is

The Trello cards can link to Notion pages; Notion pages can embed Trello boards. The combination provides the best of both paradigms without forcing either into a role it wasn't designed for.

## Final Verdict

- **Choose Trello if:** You primarily need visual workflow tracking; you have external stakeholders; you want simple, fast setup; your team thinks in kanban.

- **Choose Notion if:** You need documentation alongside tasks; you're building a team knowledge base; you want an all-in-one workspace; you have complex interconnected projects.

- **Consider both if:** Your team needs strong task visualization AND strong documentation — the combination is often more powerful than forcing either tool to do both jobs.

Neither tool is universally better. The right choice depends entirely on what you're trying to organize and how your team works.
